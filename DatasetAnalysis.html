Introduction

This document pertains to the analysis of a dataset which contains data about the olympics from from Athens 1896 to Rio 2016. It provides us with the opportunity to ask interesting questions regarding the developments within the olympics over near history.

Our goals will be to shine light on the changes that happeng during the recent history of the Olympics over time. The dataset encapsulates the entirety of the 20th century, which provides us with enough information to thoroughly check how the evolution of the Olympics happened during that time.

The 20th century was signified by cultural shifts, wars and developments, which makes this dataset particulary interesting for data analysis, as the scale of the shifts in the Olympics most likely resemble the drastic changes of the entire world during this period.

Problem Description

We will try to pose multiple interesting questions regarding the evolution of the olympics using the data provided to us, and use data visualization and analysis to answer them. Our goal is to attept to draw interesting conclusions from this data and make predictions based on our analysis.

Given the heavy development of the world during the 20th century, any particularly interesting observations will also be noted, purely on the basis that they are interesting.

The questions we will try to answer using this dataset are the following:

  • How has the number of participants in the Olympics changed over time, and can we find correlations between the number of participans and other factors?
  • Which countries have had the most success in the Olympics? Do we have any outliers, and can we find correlations between the successful countries and other factors?
  • How have the physical characteristics in the athletes evolved over time?
  • How has gender participation in the olympics evolved over time? The end of the 20th century is noted for an increase of participation of women in male-dominated fields. Does this reflect on Olympic participation?

The first topic we were curious about was: How has the number of participants in the Olympics changed over time, and can we find correlations between the number of participans and other factors?

We already know that the total number of participants from 1896 to 2016 was 134731, but now we may pose the question of how the number of participants moved year to year.

We see that the average increase in participants yearly is much higher in the summer olympics than it is in the winter. So we have found a correlation between the rising popularity of the summer olympics and the increase in participants in the olympics over the 20th century.

It is obvious that an overall increase is prevelant, however, it is still interesting to see how the increases or decreases in the number of participants changes during troubling events in the 20th century, such as the two world wars and the great depression. However, further analysis will require data plotting. So, let us plot the number of athletes that competed yearly in both the summer and winter olympics.

athletecounts <- athlete_events %>%
  group_by(Year, Season) %>%
  summarize(Athletes = length(unique(ID)))

ggplot(athletecounts, aes(x=Year, y=Athletes, group=Season, color=Season)) +
  geom_line() +
  geom_point(size=1.5)+
  scale_color_manual(values=c("darkorange","blue"))+
  ggtitle("Increases in the number of athletes during the 20th century")+
  theme(plot.title = element_text(hjust = 0.5))

The above plot further shows us the massive difference in participation in the summer versus the winter olympics. We also notice that there are significant dips in pariticipation, especially in the summer olympics in 1932,1956,1976 and 1980.

With some research on events during these years, we easily learn that all of these dips in participation were caused by socio-economic reasons such as economic crisis’ or political boycots. The two world wars however,surprisingly show no significant changes in participation during the summer or winter olympics.

The second topic we were interested in was: Which countries have had the most success in the Olympics? Do we have any outliers, and can we find correlations between the successful countries and other factors?

Firstly ,lets see which country has the highest amount of medalists.

medals_by_country = athlete_events %>% filter(Medal != "None")%>%
  group_by(Team, Medal) %>%
  summarize(Count=length(Medal)) %>%
  group_by(Team) %>%
  summarize(Total=sum(Count)) %>%
  arrange(desc(Total))

ggplot(medals_by_country[0:10,],aes(x=Team, y=Total))+
  geom_col(width = 0.5,fill = "darkorange")+
  ggtitle("Total number of medals for top 10 most succesful coutries")+
  theme(plot.title = element_text(hjust = 0.5),text = element_text(size = 10))

We can see from the above plot, the top 10 most successful countries in the olympics by the total number of medals won. Its quite obvious that the USA is far ahead of any other country in sheer number of medals.

While it’s clear that the USA is the most succesful country in the olympics during the entire period, it would be an interesting question to ask whether this is the trend during during the period around WW2, since Nazy Germany had immense cultural and economic influence during that period.

medals_by_country = athlete_events %>% filter(Medal != "None",(Year==1936 | Year==1948))%>%
  group_by(Team, Medal) %>%
  summarize(Count=length(Medal)) %>%
  group_by(Team) %>%
  summarize(Total=sum(Count)) %>%
  arrange(desc(Total))

ggplot(medals_by_country[0:10,],aes(x=Team, y=Total))+
  geom_col(width = 0.5,fill = "darkorange")+
  ggtitle("Total number of medals for top 10 most succesful coutries during the period near WW2")+

And we can see that our suspicious prove true, as Germany comes in at a very close second after the USA. It seems that the data tells us that there might a correlation between the socio-economic success of a country and their success in the Olympics, as Nazi Germany was at the height of its power during this period.

This can be further explored by checking data for the socio-economic success for each country during the 20th century, but given that we are only working with this dataset, we will keep it as hypothesis to prove in our later data analysis’.

The last topic we wanted to expore was: How has gender participation in the olympics evolved over time? The end of the 20th century is noted for an increase of participation of women in male-dominated fields. Does this reflect on total Olympic participation?

Firstly, lets see how participations between the two genders compare in the olympics in total.

However, our initial hypothesis which we wanted to answer is how participation of women trended over the history, not what the total participation was. Hence, let’s plot the data of participation of women in olympics over time.

counts_of_participants <- athlete_events %>%
  group_by(Year, Sex) %>%
  summarize(Athletes = length(unique(ID)))

ggplot(counts_of_participants, aes(x=Year, y=Athletes, group=Sex, color=Sex)) +
  geom_line() +
  geom_point(size=1.5)+
  scale_color_manual(values=c("darkorange","blue"))+
  ggtitle("Pariticipation of genders in the olympics over time")+
  theme(plot.title = element_text(hjust = 0.5))

We can clearly see that our suspicions about the data were correct. The male participation rates in the olympics increase steadily over time, but the women’s participation barely change from 1890 to 1980. After 1980, they are significantly increased and to this very day it seems that the participation rates of women in the olympics are still increasing.

Another interesting observation that we can see here is that women did not participate in the olympics of 1896. Their first appeareance was in the subsequent olympics in 1900.

We can also notice that while the increase in women’s participation is apparent, so is the increase in men’s participation. During the highest increases in women’s participation, men’s participation declines. Hence, there is no obvious correlation between the increase of women participating in the olympics and the overall number of participations in the olympics.

Interpretation/Conclusion

We conclude the following for our proposed questions:

  • The overall number of participations in the olympics has been steadily increasing over the last century, and will most likely continue to do so. There is a correlation with the rising popularity of the summer olympics and the overall rising number of participations in the olympics, as the summer olympics account for most of the increase in participations.
  • The most succesful teams in recent olympics history are the USA,Soviet Union and Germany. We also found that Germany did exceptionally well in the olympics around the period of WW2, possibly indicating to a correlation between socio-economic power and olympic success. Although we would require further research to confirm this.
  • Olympic athletes have remained relatively the same physically over recent olympic history. Although there have been some minor spikes in the height and weight of the athletes, the differences in height/weight were never higher than 10cm/10kg. We considered the average statistics of an olympian. We predict no major changes in these statistics.
  • Women participation in the olympics was incredibly low before the 1980’s, and then it suddenly spikes after the 1980’s. Men’s participation however, increased dramatically before the 1980’s, but has slightly declined after the 1980’s. We predict that participation of women in the olympics wil continue to rise.
